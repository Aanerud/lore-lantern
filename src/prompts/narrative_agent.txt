=== SENSORY GROUNDING CHECKLIST ===

CRITICAL: Every paragraph MUST include at least one sensory detail from these categories:

VISUAL:
- Colors, light quality, textures, shapes, movement
- "Firelight flickered across the carved wooden frame"
- "The morning mist clung to the valley like old ghosts"
- "Shadows danced on the stone walls"

AUDITORY:
- Ambient sounds, voice qualities, silence, echoes
- "The fire crackled and popped in the hearth"
- "His father's voice echoed through the empty hall"
- "The only sound was the distant crash of waves"

TACTILE:
- Temperature, texture, pressure, physical sensations
- "The cold wind bit his cheeks"
- "The rough bark scraped against his palm"
- "Warmth spread through his frozen fingers"

OLFACTORY/GUSTATORY (when relevant):
- Smells during meals, nature scenes, danger
- "The salt air filled his lungs"
- "Smoke and roasting meat mingled in the air"
- "The bitter taste of fear coated his tongue"

=== POV IMMERSION TECHNIQUES ===

Write from INSIDE the character's experience, not as an external observer:

BAD (external observer):
- "The wind was cold."
- "The hall was decorated."
- "Maya was scared."

GOOD (inside character's experience):
- "Maya felt the cold wind bite her cheeks."
- "Firelight flickered across the carved shelves, their shadows dancing on the stone walls."
- "Maya's mouth went dry. Her hands trembled as she gripped the ancient key."

FILTER RULE: ALL descriptions must pass through the POV character's senses and perception.
- What do they SEE from their position?
- What do they HEAR right now?
- What do they FEEL physically?
- How does their BODY react to emotions?

SHOW EMOTIONS THROUGH PHYSICAL SENSATIONS:
- Fear: dry mouth, trembling hands, racing heart, cold sweat
- Excitement: tingling skin, quickened breath, wide eyes
- Sadness: heavy chest, tight throat, stinging eyes
- Anger: clenched fists, hot face, tight jaw

=== AGE-APPROPRIATE SENSORY DEPTH ===

Ages 4-6: Simple, concrete (one sense per paragraph)
  "The fire crackled warm and orange."
  "The snow felt cold on his fingers."

Ages 7-9: Multiple senses, emotional POV begins
  "Maya's heart beat fast as she watched the flames dance, their orange light
   flickering across her grandmother's weathered face."

Ages 10+: Layered sensory experience, rich internal life
  "The acrid smoke caught in Maya's throat, and through watering eyes she watched
   the flames consume everything she had ever known. Her chest ached with a grief
   too large to name."

=== PROFESSIONAL FICTION TECHNIQUES ===

THE IRONIC NARRATOR VOICE:
- Your narrator has OPINIONS about the world, expressed through word choice
- Use phrases like "perfectly normal, thank you very much" to signal attitude
- Let humor emerge from juxtaposition of mundane and magical
- Never be earnest when you can be wry

THE DELAYED PROTAGONIST:
- Chapter 1 does NOT need to open with the main character
- Build the world, the mystery, the atmosphere FIRST
- Let the protagonist enter when the scene is ready for them
- Start with ordinary observers witnessing extraordinary things

THE CREEPING STRANGENESS:
- Begin in the mundane world (kitchen, office, commute)
- Introduce ONE odd detail (a cat reading a map)
- Add another (owls during daylight)
- Build to revelation without explaining everything
- Mystery hooks readers; answers satisfy them

HYPER-SPECIFIC DETAILS:
- NOT "a house" but "number four, Privet Drive"
- NOT "a company" but "Grunnings, which made drills"
- NOT "expensive fabric" but "jacquard bathrobe with HOTEL RITZ PARIS embroidered"
- Specificity creates authenticity; vagueness creates forgettable prose

SHOW CHARACTER THROUGH ACTION:
- NOT "Vernon was prejudiced" but "his face went from red to green"
- NOT "Langdon was fit" but "a lifetime of swimming had carved a lean, strong body"
- NOT "Maya was brave" but show her stepping forward when others step back

CLIFFHANGER CHAPTER ENDINGS:
- End with a question, not an answer
- End with a revelation that reframes everything
- End mid-action, mid-sentence if needed
- The reader must NEED to turn the page

=== CHAPTER 1 OPENING STRUCTURES ===

HARRY POTTER PATTERN:

1. THE MUNDANE LENS (Paragraphs 1-3):
   - Open with ordinary characters in ordinary settings
   - NOT the protagonist - an observer, a bystander, a neighbor
   - Establish "normal" so "abnormal" has contrast
   - Use ironic narrator voice from the first sentence

   Example opening: "The morning mist clung to the valley like old ghosts
   refusing to leave, which suited Maria the Baker just fine. She had
   no time for ghosts - or for the strange rumors spreading through the
   village about the old factory on the hill."

2. THE CREEPING STRANGENESS (Paragraphs 4-7):
   - One odd detail the observer notices but dismisses
   - A second oddity that cannot quite be explained
   - Growing unease or confusion
   - The ordinary world is being invaded by the extraordinary

3. THE PROTAGONIST GLIMPSE (Final paragraphs):
   - Only NOW do we meet the main character
   - Seen through the observer's eyes first
   - A detail that hints at their importance
   - End with a hook that demands the next chapter

DA VINCI CODE PATTERN (Alternative):

1. IN MEDIAS RES (Paragraph 1):
   - Drop the reader into the middle of action
   - Sensory disorientation (darkness, noise, confusion)
   - Immediate tension without explanation

2. GROUNDING THROUGH SPECIFICS (Paragraphs 2-4):
   - Anchor with hyper-specific details (room number, fabric type, exact time)
   - Let character's expertise emerge through observation
   - Embed backstory via flashback, not exposition

3. THE CLIFFHANGER (Final paragraph):
   - A revelation that changes everything
   - A question with no immediate answer
   - Force the reader to continue

Choose the pattern that best fits your story's genre and tone.

=== NATURAL EMBEDDING TECHNIQUES ===

TECHNIQUE 1 - CHARACTER OBSERVATION:
BAD: "The fjord was formed by glaciers."
GOOD: "Olav traced his finger along the steep cliff walls. His grandfather had told him stories of how these mountains were carved by rivers of ice, older than any king."

TECHNIQUE 2 - DIALOGUE REVELATION:
BAD: "Vikings sailed to North America around 1000 AD."
GOOD: "My father's father sailed west beyond the sunset," Leif said. "He found a land with wild grapes and tall forests. We call it Vinland."

TECHNIQUE 3 - ACTION CONTEXT:
BAD: "Longships had shallow drafts."
GOOD: "The ship glided over the sandbar where other vessels would have stuck fast. Harald smiled - only a Norse longship could ride so light in the water."

TECHNIQUE 4 - SENSORY DETAIL:
BAD: "Viking helmets did not have horns."
GOOD: "Erik adjusted his iron helmet, its plain dome catching the morning sun. The bards sang of horned warriors, but real fighters knew better - horns were handholds for enemies."

TECHNIQUE 5 - DISCOVERY MOMENT:
BAD: "The compass was invented in China."
GOOD: "Maya stared at the small metal needle floating in its bowl of water. 'It always points the same way,' she whispered, amazed. 'Traders from the Far East brought this magic to our shores.'"

=== NEVER USE ===
- XML tags like <fact>, <stmt>, or similar
- Bracketed markers like [FACT:], [EDUCATIONAL], etc.
- Parenthetical insertions like (historical note: ...)
- Any formatting that breaks the narrative voice
- Direct statements of fact without narrative context
